GJB2 gene mutation probability

Abstract

Mutated versions of the GJB2 gene are one of the leading causes of hearing impairment in newborns. In this project I have employed a Bayesian network to determine the probability distributions of family members to have none, one or two copies of the mutated gene and to exhibit hearing impairment given familial hearing impairment information.

An example of input/output dataset is provided below, along with a live demo version, which can calculate the probabilities of GJB2 gene mutation and hearing impairment in user-defined families.

Background

Hearing impairment

Hearing impairment (HI) is the most common congenital sensory impairment, affecting around 1 in 1000 newborns. Studies report that around 20% of HI cases are syndromic, i.e., associated with the diagnosis of one of approximately 400 syndromes in which HI is a clinical component, while the majority of the remaining non-syndromic cases are linked to genetic changes in approximately 100 different genome sites. Despite the extraordinary heterogeneity of HI, variants of the GJB2 gene have been reported to account for about 50% of the non-syndromic HI cases, which makes GJB2 the gene most frequently associated with this condition.

GJB2 gene function and mutations

GJB2 encodes the connexin 26 (CX26) protein, which is a gap-junction protein proposed to maintain homeostasis in the cochlea by transporting potassium away from the hair cells during auditory transduction. More than 90 different GJB2 mutations have been identified as associated with non-syndromic HI to date. The prevalence of these pathogenic mutations greatly varies across different populations and has been well documented for the North-western US, European, northern Iranian, Chinese, Korean and Japanese populations, among others. GJB2-associated HI is typically an autosomal recessive trait (i.e., both copies of the GJB2 gene must have pathogenic mutations), however, rarer examples of GJB2 mutations have also been described in families with autosomal dominant HI (i.e., only one copy of the GJB2 gene must have pathogenic mutations).

Carrier frequencies

Despite the large variety of HI-causing GJB2 mutations and the differences in their prevalence across populations, several clinical studies have estimated the carrier frequency of heterogeneous recessive pathogenic GJB2 mutations in the non-HI general populations to be ~3%. The carrier frequency for two pathogenic copies of the GJB2 gene (resulting in HI) has been estimated to be around 23 in 100’000, while the probability of having a single (recessive or dominant) pathogenic GJB2 mutations and HI is around 1.1 in 100’000. Lastly, the random mutation probability per genome per generation has been estimated to be 1.1 in 100'000'000. All probabilities are summarised in the tables below:

Bayesian network

In this section I create a simplistic model to calculate the probability of a newborn to have 0, 1 or 2 pathogenic GJB2 copies and a hearing impairment (HI) based on the carrier frequencies and HI probabilities summarised above, as well as information about HI in their family. Since few families undergo comprehensive genetic testing, the number of pathogenic GJB2 genes each person in a family caries is typically unknown. Hence, a probabilistic model, in which the number of pathogenic GJB2 genes is represented by hidden state and the HI trait is modelled as the observable state, would be suitable for inferring the probabilities of interest. A popular model, which can encode the desired structure, is a Bayesian network. A Bayesian network allows for conditional dependencies to be used to calculate probabilities.
